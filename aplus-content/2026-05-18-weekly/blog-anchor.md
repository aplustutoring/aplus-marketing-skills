# California School Dashboard 2026: Why Charter LEAs Should Lock Their Intervention Plan This Spring, Not Next Fall

The 2026 California School Dashboard will release this fall and determine which schools enter Comprehensive Support and Improvement (CSI) and Additional Targeted Support and Improvement (ATSI) for the 2026-2029 federal accountability cycle. CDE issued the Aligned Release Letter on May 5, 2026, confirming the timeline. For California charter LEAs, this is the single most consequential accountability event of the year, and the unique two-Dashboard-year eligibility rule means schools that just cleared 2025 can still be designated based on the combined 2024 plus 2025 pattern. The charter LEAs that come into November with a Tier 3 intervention program already running are not scrambling to "develop a CSI plan" once designation letters arrive. They are the plan.

## What is changing this fall

The California School Dashboard is the state's annual report card on every K-12 LEA. Under the federal Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA), Dashboard performance determines two designations that drive accountability for the next three years:

- **CSI (Comprehensive Support and Improvement):** the lowest-performing 5 percent of Title I schools statewide, plus any high school with a graduation rate below 67 percent, plus schools failing to exit a prior CSI designation
- **ATSI (Additional Targeted Support and Improvement):** schools with one or more student subgroups performing at the level that would trigger CSI if it were the schoolwide population

A school designated CSI or ATSI must develop and implement an evidence-based intervention plan, identify the resource inequities driving low performance, and submit the plan through its LEA. The current three-year cycle is in its third year, which means 2026 Dashboard determinations set the slate for the next cycle (2026-2027 through 2028-2029).

## What does this mean for California charter LEAs?

Two structural differences from a traditional district affect charter LEAs specifically.

First, the **two-Dashboard-year rule.** Per [CDE's CSI eligibility criteria](https://www.cde.ca.gov/sp/sw/t1/csi.asp), charter schools are evaluated against the same performance thresholds as districts but must meet them in two consecutive Dashboard years. The 2026 determination evaluates the combined 2025 and 2024 readings. A charter that performed at the borderline of CSI eligibility on its 2025 Dashboard can still be designated if the combined two-year pattern crosses the threshold. The implication is operational: a charter executive director cannot look at the 2025 Dashboard alone and conclude the school is safe.

Second, **the planning timeline is tighter than it looks.** Designation letters typically arrive within weeks of the Dashboard release. The plan must then be developed in partnership with educational partners, approved by the LEA, and beginning implementation by the second semester. A charter LEA without an intervention program already running enters fall scrambling to identify a vendor, sign a contract, and onboard students mid-year. A charter with a Tier 3 program already running has the plan ready to submit.

## Why the spring timing matters more than the fall designation

The decision window is now, not November. Three reasons.

The vendor procurement cycle inside a California charter LEA typically runs 4 to 8 weeks from initial scope conversation to executed agreement. A program that starts in October requires a contract executed in August at the latest, which requires a vendor decision in June or July. A charter director who waits for the designation letter to begin the procurement process is already a month behind the calendar.

Stakeholder roundtables take time. The CSI improvement plan must be developed in collaboration with parents, teachers, the school board, and the LEA's continuous improvement partner (typically the county office of education). Building that consensus mid-fall, while the school is also onboarding students into the intervention, is the procurement equivalent of building a plane in flight.

Evidence accumulates slowly. A Tier 3 intervention program that begins in October has roughly four months of student data by the end of the 2026-27 school year. A program that begins in August has eight months. By the time the LEA submits its CSI plan in winter and reports out in spring, the program with eight months of data has measurable outcomes to point to. The October program has anecdotes.

## What does the research say about intervention design that holds up under CSI review?

Stanford's National Student Support Accelerator (NSSA), led by Susanna Loeb at the Stanford Graduate School of Education, has documented the conditions under which high-impact tutoring effect sizes hold up across replication studies: at least three sessions per week, small group size of one to four students, sessions delivered during the school day, and the same tutor across the cycle. These are the conditions a CSI evidence-based intervention plan must satisfy to qualify as Tier 1 or Tier 2 evidence under ESSA's evidence hierarchy.

NWEA's most recent recovery analysis tracked more than 5 million students across the country. Per K-12 Dive's coverage, only one in seven schools have recovered in both math and reading post-pandemic. The "Rebounder" schools NWEA identified share a profile that matches the NSSA criteria exactly: consistent intervention, school-day delivery, MAP-aligned dose, stable provider. That profile is the operational definition of the kind of program that survives a CSI plan review and produces measurable outcomes within the three-year accountability cycle.

The Learning Policy Institute, founded by Linda Darling-Hammond who currently serves as President of the California State Board of Education, has long argued that intervention quality is a function of program design and staffing stability, not categorical funding source or label. The corollary: a charter LEA that builds the right intervention program before any designation letter arrives is positioned to satisfy CSI review regardless of whether they end up designated this cycle.

## What works inside a CSI improvement plan

The intervention models that survive a CSI plan review share three features. They are evidence-based under ESSA's hierarchy, which means they meet at least Tier 3 evidence (a logic model plus ongoing study) and ideally Tier 1 or Tier 2 (a randomized or quasi-experimental study showing improved outcomes). They are documented under MTSS so the Tier 2 and Tier 3 caseload is identified through CAASPP, ELPAC, and NWEA MAP data rather than teacher referral alone. And they are scoped so the LEA can demonstrate fidelity through session logs, attendance records, and pre/post outcome data.

Synchronous online small-group tutoring fits this profile cleanly. The same design features that produce learning gains also create the documentation paper trail a CSI plan submission requires.

## What charter directors and coordinators can do this month

Pull the school's 2025 California School Dashboard. Identify any indicators in the orange or red performance bands, especially in the student subgroups that drive ATSI designation (English learners, students with disabilities, low-income students, foster youth, Black, Latino, and Pacific Islander students). Compare the 2025 reading against the 2024 reading. If both years are weak on the same indicator, the school is exposed to designation regardless of the 2026 result.

Identify the student cohort that would receive [Tier 2 and Tier 3 small-group instruction](https://wetutorathome.com/services) under an intervention plan. The cohort size determines vendor scope and budget.

Begin vendor scope conversations now. A program that needs to be operational by October requires a contract signed in August, which requires a vendor decision in June or July. The fall designation letter will not slow the calendar down.
