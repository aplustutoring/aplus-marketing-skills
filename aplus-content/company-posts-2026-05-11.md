## Post 1 — "Getting Down to Facts" Report

**Topic:** 100+ researchers call for California to scale proven tutoring statewide
**Word count:** 247

The new "Getting Down to Facts" report runs more than 1,000 pages and represents the work of 100+ K-12 researchers. The lead voices behind it: Linda Darling-Hammond (president of the California State Board of Education) and Susanna Loeb (Stanford).

The report's recommendations will sound familiar to anyone running a charter intervention program. The state could require districts to adopt curricula with evidence behind them. Hire more tutors and counselors. Expand after-school programs. Stop letting every district reinvent the wheel.

It names California's local-control model as the reason interventions that work in one school never reach the next one. Special programs coordinators and charter directors have been navigating that fragmentation for years, building (or buying) intervention from scratch because the state never centralized what works.

This is a research-backed validation of the model we already see succeeding in our partner schools: high-dosage, human-led tutoring delivered through a specialist partner instead of built in-house. Our iLEAD Math Tier 3 cohort tells the same story at the program level: 12 students, 17 average hours of one-to-one instruction, 9 of 12 students with positive RIT growth, average gain of 20.8 percentile points across the cohort.

The researchers are now saying what the schools doing the work have been saying. If your district is reviewing intervention strategy for 2026-27, the full report is worth your team's time.

Source: CalMatters, May 7, 2026

## Post 2 — NWEA Dashboard

**Topic:** Reading recovery stalled, math gains modest — what the public data reveals
**Word count:** 245

NWEA's new national dashboard pulls MAP Growth data from more than 7 million students across 20,000 schools. The Spring 2025 trend snapshot makes one fact undeniable: reading recovery has stalled.

Math is recovering modestly. Brookings analysis of five years of post-pandemic test data puts math on track for full recovery in about seven years at the current pace. Reading is worse: four of five major assessments show continued declines from 2022 to 2024. The recovery that is happening is not even either. Hispanic and economically disadvantaged students are still recovering more slowly than peers.

For Title I and Title III coordinators reviewing year-end MAP data, the headline matches what your screen is probably showing: math is moving, reading is not, and the gap is still widest for the students who needed gains most.

The national average is the result of what most schools are doing. It is not a ceiling for what your school can do. The partner schools that built consistent, small-group ELA intervention into the MTSS framework see different results than the dashboard suggests: small groups, trained specialist tutors, regular dosage, RIT-aligned progress monitoring.

The pattern is repeatable. Reading recovery is not happening on its own, and the dashboard now makes that fact public.

If your team has not pulled the most recent trend snapshot, the dashboard is open to anyone.

Sources: NWEA Spring 2025 Trend Snapshot; Brookings (2026); K-12 Dive (Oct 2025)

## Post 3 — AB 2555 (English Learner Auto-Reclassification)

**Topic:** AB 2555 would auto-reclassify ELs who score 4 on the ELPAC
**Word count:** 242

AB 2555 is moving through the California legislature with a deceptively simple proposal: a student who scores 4 (the highest level) on the ELPAC should be automatically reclassified as English proficient.

The math behind the bill: about 18,500 California students per year score 4 on the ELPAC and remain classified as English Learners anyway. Stanford researchers found that 39% of students who score 4 are not reclassified the following year. The bottleneck is the local "basic skills" criterion that lets a single below-grade-level ELA score keep a fluent student in EL status.

For special programs coordinators managing LTEL caseloads under Title III, this is the policy version of what you see on the ground: students who are fluent in English, sitting in EL intervention because the system has not caught up to their progress.

AB 2555 removes the bureaucratic barrier. The skills gap that keeps some of these students from clearing the local cut score is what targeted intervention solves.

Our ELA intervention with LTEL students sits in that gap. We pair specialist tutors with small groups, run progress monitoring against state-aligned benchmarks, document reclassification readiness for the coordinator's evidence file, and align scope of work to Title III plan goals.

If your team has Title III dollars allocated for LTEL intervention this year, AB 2555 makes the case for moving on it now. Worth tracking on EdSource.

Sources: EdSource (March 2026); AB 2555 bill text; Stanford research via PACE (March 2026)
